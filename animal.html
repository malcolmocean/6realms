the animal realm

"He is intoxicated with his safe, self-contained, familiar world and so fixes his attention on familiar goals and pursues them with unswerving and stubborn determination." — Chögyam Trungpa, Cutting Through Spiritual Materialism

identifying with what I do have → animal realm

This is the quietest trap on the wheel, and the one it's rudest to name, because from inside it looks like virtue: keeping your head down, doing the work, not making a fuss. Nothing hurts much. Nothing is questioned, either. The realm's poison isn't cruelty or craving — it's the refusal to look up.

novel

Animal Farm — George Orwell

Boxer the cart-horse meets every catastrophe by lowering his head and pulling. His whole answer to the world, and the realm's motto:

I will work harder!

— Boxer's maxim — devotion as blindness, right up to the knacker's van

poem

Toads — Philip Larkin

Why should I let the toad work Squat on my life? Can't I use my wit as a pitchfork And drive the brute off?

— The Less Deceived, 1955

He can't, it turns out — the poem ends admitting something toad-like squats in him too. Larkin worked the library desk another thirty years. The honesty is the point: the realm is comfortable, and part of us wants it.

films

Groundhog Day — dir. Harold Ramis · Office Space — dir. Mike Judge

Two documentaries about the same territory. The early loops of Groundhog Day — the same day lived on autopilot before it's lived awake — and the cubicle grazing-lands of Office Space, TPS reports at the feed-trough. Note that Phil Connors only gets out of the loop when he stops trying to escape it and starts paying attention inside it.

song

Once in a Lifetime — Talking Heads

The realm's anthem: a man surfaces for one dazed moment in the middle of his own life — beautiful house, beautiful wife, how did I get here? — while the chorus chants the lullaby that put him under: same as it ever was, same as it ever was.

essay

The Myth of Sisyphus — Albert Camus

The boulder, the hill, forever — the realm's floor plan. But Camus's famous last sentence ("one must imagine Sisyphus happy") is a wager the animal realm can't take: happiness through full consciousness of the routine, not through never noticing it.
