poems for the realms — working page

What this is: candidate poems from poetryfoundation.org for each realm, across the same six lenses as films.html: native wisdom (the virtue living in the realm's own energy, keyed to Romeo's may-2023 thread), exit door (the poem that sees through the realm's mechanism), trap (the deepening pit — the Requiem-for-a-Dream axis), seduction (why you'd walk in willingly), inhabiting (ordinary life at the realm's milder levels), the witness (the realm's beings beheld with compassion — the Buddha-who-appears-in-each-realm's eye). 1–2 poems per cell, ranked HIGH / SOLID / TENTATIVE (= how satisfied I am that this is the answer, not just an answer).

How I searched: Poetry Foundation has no "top 100" list. What it has: (1) topic facets on /poems/browse — 13 subjects, 13 emotions (anger, boredom, contentment, despair, etc.) — but default sort is newest-first, so it surfaces recent magazine poems; adding &sortBy=prod_PFONE_post_date_asc surfaces the classic archive; (2) curated collections (used "Poems of Protest, Resistance, and Empowerment" and "Poetry and Food"); (3) their search index, which I queried directly to verify every URL below. So: canon picks verified on-site, plus a few finds from the facet/collection sweeps. A famous-poems tag sweep is at the bottom.

Wanted but not on the site (rights, presumably): Wendell Berry "The Peace of Wild Things" (would be animal-wisdom HIGH), Mary Oliver "Wild Geese" (animal-wisdom HIGH) and "The Summer Day" (animal exit door — "what is it you plan to do..."), Jane Kenyon "Otherwise" (animal-inhabiting HIGH), Dylan Thomas "Fern Hill" (deva-trap HIGH), Auden "Musée des Beaux Arts" (deva-witness HIGH — the gods'-eye indifference to Icarus, witnessed), Rilke "The Panther" (animal-trap HIGH; his "Archaic Torso of Apollo" is on the site and holds the animal exit door below), Bishop "The Fish" (animal-witness HIGH), Levertov "O Taste and See" (animal exit). If we're willing to link off-site, several are the single best fit for their cell.

hell
(suffering is real and matters; righteous anger; the worthlessness snarl)

London — William Blake — HIGH.
"the mind-forg'd manacles I hear"
Walks the city and refuses to look away from any of it: every face marked, every cry counted. The first noble truth as street reportage — and "mind-forg'd manacles" is exactly "not chosen but conditioned."

won't you celebrate with me — Lucille Clifton — HIGH.
The wisdom side of hell from someone who was in it: the suffering was real, it mattered, and the fact of survival is itself the celebration. Righteous without cruelty. (Found via the protest collection.)

Love (III) — George Herbert — HIGH.
"Love bade me welcome. Yet my soul drew back, / Guilty of dust and sin."
The exit from the worthlessness snarl itself: the guest insists he's too stained to be looked at, and hospitality declines the argument — "You must sit down, says Love, and taste my meat." Hell's sealing layer ("my suffering is my fault") dissolved not by rebuttal but by being seated and fed. This was the poem Simone Weil said carried her through.

My own heart let me more have pity on — Gerard Manley Hopkins — HIGH.
The same poet who wrote the trap poem (one row over) writes the door: stop interrogating the torment, "call off thoughts awhile," leave comfort root-room. Self-compassion prescribed from inside hell by its most credentialed resident.

No worst, there is none — Gerard Manley Hopkins — HIGH.
"O the mind, mind has mountains; cliffs of fall / Frightful, sheer, no-man-fathomed"
The pit, literally: each pitch of grief opens onto a worse one, wrung on an "age-old anvil." The most exact deepening-pit poem I know of on the site.

Lady Lazarus — Sylvia Plath — SOLID.
The snarl where suffering becomes identity and performance at once — dying as "an art" done "exceptionally well," with an audience. The ownership layer sealing the suffering in.

The Raven — Edgar Allan Poe — HIGH.
The narrator knows the bird only says "Nevermore" — and keeps asking it questions whose answers will hurt more. Despair as a room you keep choosing, lamplight and all. That's the seduction: grief made gorgeous and self-administered.

Not Waving but Drowning — Stevie Smith — HIGH.
The core hell feeling ("your suffering doesn't matter / isn't even perceived") at ordinary social distance: everyone thought he was larking. "Much too far out all my life" — hell inhabited quietly, for decades, while seeming fine.

Aubade — Philip Larkin — SOLID.
Functional daytime life with the 4 a.m. dread as a fixed roommate. Hell's milder level: you still go to work; the edge of the pit is just always there before dawn.

Strange Meeting — Wilfred Owen — HIGH.
"I am the enemy you killed, my friend."
Set in hell itself — "down some profound dull tunnel" — where the killed enemy greets his killer without rancor and mourns "the pity of war, the pity war distilled." Hell's beings beholding each other with the compassion the realm is defined by lacking. The column's whole idea, from inside.

The Wound-Dresser — Walt Whitman — HIGH.
Bed to bed among the maimed of both sides, "with hinged knees and steady hand," dressing what can't heal. The witness as a practice with hours: unflinching, unranking, tender to the end of the ward.
hungry ghost
(craving; contracted awareness; never enough; compassion for those who lack)

Ithaka — C. P. Cavafy — SOLID.
Wanting, held wisely: pray the road is long; the destination's job was to cause the journey, and arriving "poor" is not being cheated. Seeking outward with the grasping metabolized — preta wisdom rather than preta symptom.

Ulysses — Alfred, Lord Tennyson — SOLID.
"To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield"
"Not content with merely what is given" ennobled into a life. Caveat: read cold, Ulysses also abandons his actual kingdom and family for the horizon — the poem sits right on the line between preta wisdom and preta rationalization, which honestly makes it a better exhibit.

The Pulley — George Herbert — HIGH.
"If goodness lead him not, yet weariness / May toss him to my breast."
The mechanism seen through completely: every blessing poured out except rest, so that "repining restlessness" is the one thing that can't be satisfied by acquisition — because it was never aimed at objects. The wanting itself re-read as the homing signal. Preta's exit door in eighteen lines.

The World Is Too Much With Us — William Wordsworth — SOLID.
"Getting and spending, we lay waste our powers" — the diagnosis from just outside the store. SOLID rather than HIGH because the poem only wishes for the exit (better a pagan glimpsing Proteus) rather than finding it.

Goblin Market — Christina Rossetti — HIGH.
"I ate and ate my fill, / Yet my mouth waters still"
The full Requiem arc in fairy-tale form: first taste free, then the craving that can't be re-fed, the wasting, the kicking of the habit only through someone else absorbing the harm. The site's definitive addiction poem.

Blackberry-Picking — Seamus Heaney — HIGH.
Lust, hoarding, rot, and doing it again every year knowing the sweetness won't keep. The trap's engine in miniature: the wanting survives every disappointment intact. (Found via the food collection.)

La Belle Dame sans Merci — John Keats — HIGH.
"And there she lulled me asleep"
The taste of honey-wild and manna-dew, then waking starving on the cold hillside with the other pale kings who all got the same treatment. The seduction and the withdrawal in one ballad — Requiem's other half.

A Supermarket in California — Allen Ginsberg — HIGH.
Wandering the aisles of total abundance at night, hungry, shopping for images, buying nothing. The mild everyday preta level: not depraved, just never quite fed by any of it — and tender toward everyone else in the store.

The Bean Eaters — Gwendolyn Brooks — HIGH.
An old couple who lack — "Dinner is a casual affair," tin flatware, a rented back room — beheld with such tenderness that the poverty and the remembering ("twinklings and twinges") both keep their dignity. Compassion for those who lack, exactly as the realm's wisdom tweet names it.

To a Poor Old Woman — William Carlos Williams — HIGH.
A woman munching plums from a paper bag, and the poem repeats "they taste good to her" three ways until you believe it. Appetite witnessed as solace rather than judged as craving — the ghost's opposite, offered to a person the world would otherwise not see at all.
animal
(safety; comfort and hospitality enabling practice; awareness of space; low on concepts)

The Pasture — Robert Frost — SOLID.
"I sha'n't be gone long.—You come too."
Hospitality at its most animal: chores, water, a calf, and the repeated invitation. Comfort offered so simply it doubles as an invitation to practice. Caveat: slight; it's wisdom by demonstration rather than statement.

Song of Myself, §32 — Walt Whitman — SOLID.
"I think I could turn and live with animals... They do not sweat and whine about their condition"
The classic statement of what animals have that we lost — no worthlessness snarl ("not one kneels to another"), no preta ("not one is demented with the mania of owning things"). The animal realm's virtues seen from the human realm, which is exactly what the wisdom lens wants. (Link is to the whole poem; section 32.)

Archaic Torso of Apollo — Rainer Maria Rilke — HIGH.
Fourteen lines of looking at a broken statue that turns out to be looking at you, and then the sentence the whole column exists for: "You must change your life." The exit from stupor is being seen — attention arriving from outside the routine and making the comfortable life suddenly insufficient. (The Panther isn't on the site; this is, and it's the stronger door anyway.)

The Windhover — Gerard Manley Hopkins — SOLID.
"My heart in hiding / Stirred for a bird"
The exit built from the realm's own materials: a creature's total mastery of space cracks open the watcher's safety ("in hiding") — awareness of space upgraded from vigilance to awe.

Lying in a Hammock at William Duffy's Farm... — James Wright — HIGH.
Twelve lines of perfect creaturely ease — butterfly, cowbells, horse droppings, hawk — and then the door of the trap clicks: "I have wasted my life." Whether that last line is the trap speaking or the wisdom noticing the trap is the argument the poem wants you to have. Best animal-trap poem available on the site.

The Lotos-eaters — Alfred, Lord Tennyson — HIGH.
"surely, surely, slumber is more sweet than toil"
The sales pitch for the animal realm, delivered in the most luxurious meter Tennyson could build: why strive, why remember, why go home at all when there's the warm beach and the fruit. Cockaigne's exact poem — pairs with the Bruegel on the animal page.

Osso Buco — Billy Collins — SOLID.
Well-fed contentment, described from inside without apology — while staying just aware enough of the world's hunger to make it animal-realm rather than deva. The mild level: a good dinner, a warm house, sleep.

Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening — Robert Frost — SOLID.
Pure spatial awareness and creaturely pull — the dark woods, the snow, the horse who knows the routine — with "promises to keep" tugging from the human realm. Living on the animal/human border, which is where most of us keep our animal realm.

To a Mouse — Robert Burns — HIGH.
"I'm truly sorry man's dominion, / Has broken nature's social union"
A field creature's ruined nest, and a plowman stopping mid-furrow to apologize to it as a "fellow-mortal." The realm witnessed with tenderness and clear sight of the difference: the mouse is blessed by the present moment; the man drags guesses-and-fears both directions in time.

from Jubilate Agno (the Jeoffry section) — Christopher Smart — HIGH.
A cat's morning routine beheld, from inside an asylum, as "the servant of the Living God duly and daily serving him" — every stretch and wash counted as worship. The animal realm's daily round witnessed so lovingly it becomes liturgy.
human
(right mix of suffering and pleasure; end of ignorance; joy of understanding)

On First Looking into Chapman's Homer — John Keats — HIGH.
"like some watcher of the skies / When a new planet swims into his ken"
The joy of understanding, caught at the exact moment ignorance ends. Nothing else on the site states the human realm's wisdom this purely.

The Darkling Thrush — Thomas Hardy — TENTATIVE.
Bleak century's end, and a thrush singing of "some blessed Hope, whereof he knew / And I was unaware." Tentative because it's about the limit of understanding — hope arriving as evidence you don't yet have. Human realm at its most Melencolia-I.

As Kingfishers Catch Fire — Gerard Manley Hopkins — HIGH.
Each thing "selves — goes itself," and then the turn: the same selving, seen truly, is "Christ play[ing] in ten thousand places." The end of ignorance rendered as a change in what seeing is. films.html calls the human exit door "maybe unfilmable" — this cell is where poetry quietly wins the comparison; the devotional lyric is practically the exit-door genre.

Tintern Abbey — William Wordsworth — SOLID.
"A sense sublime / Of something far more deeply interfused" — and, crucially, the payout in conduct: "little, nameless, unremembered, acts / Of kindness and of love." Understanding that cashes out as how you treat people.

The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock — T. S. Eliot — HIGH.
"there will be time / To wonder, 'Do I dare?' and, 'Do I dare?'"
The specifically human pit: self-consciousness recursing until action is impossible. A hundred visions and revisions, the overwhelming question never asked. The realm's gift (reflection) become its trap.

First Fig — Edna St. Vincent Millay — HIGH.
"My candle burns at both ends... it gives a lovely light!"
Four lines of why being human — mortal, doomed, intense — feels glamorous from inside. The seduction of the "right mix": enough suffering to make the pleasure mean something.

This Is Just To Say — William Carlos Williams — HIGH.
Ordinary appetite, minor transgression, apology-that-isn't, and the plums were delicious. Daily human life at the scale it's actually lived — desire and conscience negotiating over the icebox.

Birches — Robert Frost — SOLID.
"Earth's the right place for love: / I don't know where it's likely to go better."
Wanting to leave earth and be brought back — climbing toward heaven, not to it. The turtle-yoke teaching in a New England accent: this is the realm where the mix is right, so stay.

Crossing Brooklyn Ferry — Walt Whitman — HIGH.
"I am with you, you men and women of a generation, or ever so many generations hence"
Every commuter who will ever cross the river, beheld with love across a century — doubts, dark patches, and all ("I too knitted the old knot of contrariety"). The human realm witnessed en masse, without one soul ranked above another.

The Whitsun Weddings — Philip Larkin — SOLID.
Wedding parties boarding a train, watched by a bachelor who came to mock and ends somewhere near blessing — "a sense of falling, like an arrow-shower." Witness with English reluctance: the tenderness leaks in past the irony, which may make it more trustworthy.
titans
(pride, envy, contest; coordination, shared defense, competition calling you to your best self)

To be of use — Marge Piercy — HIGH.
People who "jump into work head first," who "harness themselves, an ox to a heavy cart" — coordination for things beyond you, submersion in the common task. The titan wisdom with the pride burned off.

Let America Be America Again — Langston Hughes — HIGH.
Calling the callous to task at national scale while refusing to abandon the shared value being betrayed — critique and allegiance in the same breath. Shared defense of shared values, exactly.

Ozymandias — Percy Bysshe Shelley — HIGH.
"Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!"
The whole comparison economy audited by sand: the sneer outlives the empire, the pedestal outlives the works, and the only thing that actually traveled through time is the sculptor's seeing. The mechanism (rank, monument, rivalry) exposed as already ruined. (Promoted here from the tag sweep.)

I'm Nobody! Who are you? — Emily Dickinson — HIGH.
The exit taken cheerfully: declining the whole frog-economy of status ("How public – like a Frog –") and finding a conspirator on the other side of it. The shortest possible walk out of the titan realm.

Richard Cory — Edwin Arlington Robinson — HIGH.
Status envy's whole optics in sixteen lines: the glitter from below, the hollowness above, and no information passing between them until the last line. The comparison economy where everyone loses.

My Last Duchess — Robert Browning — SOLID.
Pride at the level where another person's un-ranked warmth ("she liked whate'er she looked on") reads as insubordination and gets "stopped." The demon-realm endpoint: control replacing relationship entirely.

Invictus — William Ernest Henley — HIGH.
"I am the master of my fate, / I am the captain of my soul"
Genuinely thrilling — that's the point. Unconquerable sovereign selfhood is the titan realm's best offer, and the poem shows why people take the deal. (Note the realm-tell: even the exit-shaped gods of this poem are "whatever gods may be," addressed as rivals.)

The Charge of the Light Brigade — Alfred, Lord Tennyson — SOLID.
The glory of coordinated sacrifice, gorgeous and unquestioning — "theirs not to reason why" as a feature. Seduction by honor; the poem half-knows someone had blundered and salutes anyway.

Mending Wall — Robert Frost — HIGH.
The mild daily titan level: boundary maintenance as an annual neighborly ritual, each keeping the wall between them as they go. Not war — just the low-grade territorial vigilance of "good fences," inherited and never re-examined.

To an Athlete Dying Young — A. E. Housman — SOLID.
The competitor carried shoulder-high twice — once for the record, once in the coffin — beheld with an elegist's tenderness and zero illusions about how long glory keeps ("the name died before the man"). The titan witnessed at the finish line, loved anyway.

The Convergence of the Twain — Thomas Hardy — TENTATIVE.
The Titanic and its iceberg grown for each other, witnessed with cosmic coolness. Tentative as witness because Hardy's gaze is icy rather than compassionate — it may belong in the exit-door cell as a mechanism poem, or nowhere. Flagging for your read.
deva
(bliss; appreciating experience; stabilizing; reveling in creation; flips into hell when it runs out)

Pied Beauty — Gerard Manley Hopkins — HIGH.
"Glory be to God for dappled things"
Appreciation as an act of praise for creation itself — dappled, fickle, freckled particulars, referred upward to what "fathers-forth." Appreciating experience and reveling in creation in eleven lines.

From Blossoms — Li-Young Lee — SOLID.
Peaches taken fully in — "joy to joy... blossom to impossible blossom." SOLID instead of HIGH only because its famous close (living "as if death were nowhere in the background") is the deva trap stated as the wish — the poem knows it, which is the wisdom.

Ode to a Nightingale — John Keats — HIGH.
"the fancy cannot cheat so well / As she is fam'd to do"
An actual round trip: flies into the deathless blissful realm on "the viewless wings of Poesy," feels the pull to dissolve there ("half in love with easeful Death"), and is tolled back to his sole self with the realm's spell broken. The exit door used, from the inside, with a full trip report.

Ode on a Grecian Urn — John Keats — HIGH.
The deva realm inspected at leisure: figures forever young, love "for ever warm," and the verdict slipped in at the turn — "Cold Pastoral!" Changeless bliss is frozen bliss; the boughs never shed because nothing there is alive.

Sunday Morning — Wallace Stevens — HIGH.
"complacencies of the peignoir, and late / Coffee and oranges in a sunny chair"
Opens inside the deva realm — comfort holding off "the dark encroachment of that old catastrophe" — and then spends seven stanzas showing why a paradise without change can't satisfy: "Death is the mother of beauty." The diagnosis of the realm, written from its most comfortable chair.

Kubla Khan — Samuel Taylor Coleridge — HIGH.
"For he on honey-dew hath fed, / And drunk the milk of Paradise"
The pleasure-dome decreed: creation as pure sovereign delight, and the visionary glamour of the one who's tasted it. That the vision famously broke off mid-composition is the deva realm's whole biography.

Sailing to Byzantium — William Butler Yeats — SOLID.
The wish to be gathered "into the artifice of eternity" — traded out of the dying-animal body into golden, singing permanence. The deva realm advertised to the aging from the human shore; the Grecian Urn's "Cold Pastoral" is the answer it doesn't want.

I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud — William Wordsworth — HIGH.
"the bliss of solitude"
Mild resident-level deva: bliss encountered, then banked — the daffodils paying out later wealth whenever wanted. Appreciation with a stabilizing savings account.

I dwell in Possibility (466) — Emily Dickinson — SOLID.
Living in the house of creation — more windows, superior doors, visitors the fairest — "spreading wide my narrow Hands / To gather Paradise." Reveling in creative capacity as a dwelling, at humane scale.

The Wild Swans at Coole — William Butler Yeats — HIGH.
"Their hearts have not grown old"
Fifty-nine unwearied creatures, "brilliant," paired, lifting off in great broken rings — beheld by a watcher counting his own autumns. The deva realm's beings witnessed from the human shore with ache and without resentment; he even blesses whoever's eyes they'll delight after he's gone. (Deva-witness was the weakest cell in films.html too — Musée des Beaux Arts would compete for this seat if it were on the site.)

tag sweep — famous poems, quick realm tags

Since there's no site-native top-100, this is the standard most-anthologized set (nearly all on PF), tagged the way you suggested. Format: [tag] — reasoning in a phrase.

  • Song of Myself — Whitman — [all]: deliberately tours every realm and identifies with each ("I am the man, I suffer'd, I was there"); the bodhisattva move done in free verse.
  • The Waste Land — Eliot — [all]: hell (unreal city), preta (the typist scene's joyless appetite), animal (stupor), titans (falling towers), deva-in-ruins; ends reaching for the exit in Sanskrit.
  • Auguries of Innocence — Blake — [all]: "joy and woe are woven fine" is the realms' moral physics stated as couplets.
  • Harlem — Hughes — [preta/hell]: deferred wanting festering through every simile toward explosion.
  • Ozymandias — Shelley — [titans] → promoted into the grid above (titans exit door).
  • The Second Coming — Yeats — [titans/hell]: coordination failing ("the centre cannot hold"), conviction maldistributed, something pitiless arriving.
  • The Tyger — Blake — [deva/hell]: reveling in creation, aimed at creation's terrifying half; the deva realm forced to look at hell and asked "did he who made the Lamb make thee?"
  • Do Not Go Gentle into That Good Night — Dylan Thomas — [hell/human]: righteous rage recommended as the correct response to dying — hell-wisdom deployed at the human realm's defining fact.
  • The Road Not Taken — Frost — [human]: choice, and the story-making about choice ("I shall be telling this with a sigh") — human-realm narrative machinery caught running.
  • Because I could not stop for Death (479) — Dickinson — [human]: mortality as a civil carriage ride through the whole arc of a life; the human realm's defining companion, met with courtesy.
  • "Hope" is the thing with feathers (314) — Dickinson — [human]: hope that never asks a crumb — pointedly not preta; the human realm's renewable resource.
  • Wild nights - Wild nights! (269) — Dickinson — [preta/deva]: luxury-craving, but "done with the compass" — desire imagining its own completion, which preta never actually reaches.
  • The Red Wheelbarrow — Williams — [none]: pure attention with no grasping, no aversion, no self in it; arguably what any realm looks like the moment you actually just look.

notes for iteration

  • The exit-door column is poetry's home turf, inverting films.html's finding. films calls the human exit door "maybe unfilmable"; in poetry it's a whole genre — the devotional lyric. And George Herbert turns out to be the exit-door poet the way Miyazaki is the realm-wisdom filmmaker: Love (III) for hell, The Pulley for preta, and The Collar (not used above; poems/44360) does the same move for titan-flavored rebellion — rage building for thirty lines until one word ("Child!") ends it. Three realms, same door, same poet.
  • Keats owns the deva exit: Nightingale is the round trip, Grecian Urn the inspection report. Between them and Sunday Morning, deva has the strongest see-through material of any realm — maybe because poets keep applying for the job of god and filing honest rejections.
  • The witness column filled instantly and at high quality (Strange Meeting, Wound-Dresser, Bean Eaters, To a Mouse, Jeoffry, Crossing Brooklyn Ferry, Wild Swans) — same phenomenon as films.html, where it absorbed five benched masterpieces. Elegy and apostrophe are witness-shaped forms; poetry has been running this column for centuries.
  • Weakest cells now: titans-witness (Housman is close but elegizes the athlete, not quite the striving; Hardy is cold — flagged), animal-wisdom (still missing Berry/Oliver/Kenyon — see the not-on-site list), preta-wisdom (Cavafy/Tennyson both shade into other realms). Cells left at one poem on purpose: hell-seduction, animal-trap, preta-seduction/inhabiting, human-trap/seduction, titans-inhabiting, deva-trap — happy with the single occupant in each.
  • Cells I'd defend hardest: hell-trap + hell-exit (the two Hopkins/Herbert pairs mirror each other), preta-exit (The Pulley), preta-trap (Rossetti + Heaney), animal-exit (Archaic Torso), deva-exit (the Keats odes), deva-trap (Sunday Morning).
  • The emotion facets exist for future sweeps: anger, anxiety-insecurity, blame-guilt, boredom, disappointment, gratitude, grief, hope-optimism, humor, joy-contentment, loneliness, melancholy-despair, passion.
