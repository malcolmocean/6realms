poems for the realms — working page

What this is: candidate poems from poetryfoundation.org for each realm, across four lenses: wisdom (the realm's wisdom/truth, keyed to the may-2023 tweet thread), trap (the deepening pit — the Requiem-for-a-Dream axis), seduction (why you'd walk in willingly), inhabiting (what it's like to live at the realm's milder levels). 1–2 poems per cell, ordered by confidence: ★★★ = confident this belongs, ★★ = good fit with caveats, ★ = tentative/interesting.

How I searched: Poetry Foundation has no "top 100" list. What it has: (1) topic facets on /poems/browse — 13 subjects, 13 emotions (anger, boredom, contentment, despair, desire under Love, etc.) — but default sort is newest-first, so it surfaces recent magazine poems; adding &sortBy=prod_PFONE_post_date_asc surfaces the classic archive; (2) curated collections (used "Poems of Protest, Resistance, and Empowerment" and "Poetry and Food"); (3) their search index, which I queried directly to verify every URL below. So: canon picks verified on-site, plus a few finds from the facet/collection sweeps. A famous-poems tag sweep is at the bottom.

Wanted but not on the site (rights, presumably): Wendell Berry "The Peace of Wild Things" (would be animal-wisdom ★★★), Mary Oliver "Wild Geese" (animal-wisdom ★★★) and "The Summer Day" (animal/human), Jane Kenyon "Otherwise" (animal-inhabiting ★★★), Dylan Thomas "Fern Hill" (deva-trap ★★★), Auden "Musée des Beaux Arts" (deva-trap ★★★ — the gods'-eye indifference to Icarus), Rilke "The Panther" (animal-trap ★★★). If we're willing to link off-site for these, several are the single best fit for their cell.

wisdom / truth the trap / deepening pit the seductiveness inhabiting the milder levels
hell
(suffering is real and matters; righteous anger; the worthlessness snarl)		 ★★★ London — William Blake
"the mind-forg'd manacles I hear"
Walks the city and refuses to look away from any of it: every face marked, every cry counted. The first noble truth as street reportage — and "mind-forg'd manacles" is exactly "not chosen but conditioned."

★★★ won't you celebrate with me — Lucille Clifton
The wisdom side of hell from someone who was in it: the suffering was real, it mattered, and the fact of survival is itself the celebration. Righteous without cruelty. (Found via the protest collection.) 		★★★ No worst, there is none — Gerard Manley Hopkins
"O the mind, mind has mountains; cliffs of fall / Frightful, sheer, no-man-fathomed"
The pit, literally: each pitch of grief opens onto a worse one, wrung on an "age-old anvil." The most exact deepening-pit poem I know of on the site.

★★ Lady Lazarus — Sylvia Plath
The snarl where suffering becomes identity and performance at once — dying as "an art" done "exceptionally well," with an audience. The ownership layer sealing the suffering in. 		★★★ The Raven — Edgar Allan Poe
The narrator knows the bird only says "Nevermore" — and keeps asking it questions whose answers will hurt more. Despair as a room you keep choosing, lamplight and all. That's the seduction: grief made gorgeous and self-administered. 		★★★ Not Waving but Drowning — Stevie Smith
The core hell feeling ("your suffering doesn't matter / isn't even perceived") at ordinary social distance: everyone thought he was larking. "Much too far out all my life" — hell inhabited quietly, for decades, while seeming fine.

★★ Aubade — Philip Larkin
Functional daytime life with the 4 a.m. dread as a fixed roommate. Hell's milder level: you still go to work; the edge of the pit is just always there before dawn.
hungry ghost
(craving; contracted awareness; never enough; wisdom of seeking + compassion for those who lack)		 ★★ Ithaka — C. P. Cavafy
Wanting, held wisely: pray the road is long; the destination's job was to cause the journey, and arriving "poor" is not being cheated. Seeking outward with the grasping metabolized — preta wisdom rather than preta symptom.

★★ Ulysses — Alfred, Lord Tennyson
"To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield"
"Not content with merely what is given" ennobled into a life. Caveat: read cold, Ulysses also abandons his actual kingdom and family for the horizon — the poem sits right on the line between preta wisdom and preta rationalization, which honestly makes it a better exhibit. 		★★★ Goblin Market — Christina Rossetti
"I ate and ate my fill, / Yet my mouth waters still"
The full Requiem arc in fairy-tale form: first taste free, then the craving that can't be re-fed, the wasting, the kicking of the habit only through someone else absorbing the harm. The site's definitive addiction poem.

★★★ Blackberry-Picking — Seamus Heaney
Lust, hoarding, rot, and doing it again every year knowing the sweetness won't keep. The trap's engine in miniature: the wanting survives every disappointment intact. (Found via the food collection.) 		★★★ La Belle Dame sans Merci — John Keats
"And there she lulled me asleep"
The taste of honey-wild and manna-dew, then waking starving on the cold hillside with the other pale kings who all got the same treatment. The seduction and the withdrawal in one ballad — Requiem's other half. 		★★★ A Supermarket in California — Allen Ginsberg
Wandering the aisles of total abundance at night, hungry, shopping for images, buying nothing. The mild everyday preta level: not depraved, just never quite fed by any of it — and tender toward everyone else in the store.
animal
(safety; comfort and hospitality enabling practice; awareness of space; low on concepts)		 ★★ The Pasture — Robert Frost
"I sha'n't be gone long.—You come too."
Hospitality at its most animal: chores, water, a calf, and the repeated invitation. Comfort offered so simply it doubles as an invitation to practice. Caveat: slight; it's wisdom by demonstration rather than statement.

★★ Song of Myself, §32 — Walt Whitman
"I think I could turn and live with animals... They do not sweat and whine about their condition"
The classic statement of what animals have that we lost — no worthlessness snarl ("not one kneels to another"), no preta ("not one is demented with the mania of owning things"). It's the animal realm's virtues seen from the human realm, which is exactly what the wisdom lens wants. (Link is to the whole poem; section 32.) 		★★★ Lying in a Hammock at William Duffy's Farm... — James Wright
Twelve lines of perfect creaturely ease — butterfly, cowbells, horse droppings, hawk — and then the door of the trap clicks: "I have wasted my life." Whether that last line is the trap speaking or the wisdom noticing the trap is the argument the poem wants you to have. Best animal-trap poem available on the site. 		★★★ The Lotos-eaters — Alfred, Lord Tennyson
"surely, surely, slumber is more sweet than toil"
The sales pitch for the animal realm, delivered in the most luxurious meter Tennyson could build: why strive, why remember, why go home at all when there's the warm beach and the fruit. Cockaigne's exact poem — pairs with the Bruegel on the animal page. 		★★ Osso Buco — Billy Collins
Well-fed contentment, described from inside without apology — while staying just aware enough of the world's hunger to make it animal-realm rather than deva. The mild level: a good dinner, a warm house, sleep.

★★ Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening — Robert Frost
Pure spatial awareness and creaturely pull — the dark woods, the snow, the horse who knows the routine — with "promises to keep" tugging from the human realm. Living on the animal/human border, which is where most of us keep our animal realm.
human
(right mix of suffering and pleasure; end of ignorance; joy of understanding)		 ★★★ On First Looking into Chapman's Homer — John Keats
"like some watcher of the skies / When a new planet swims into his ken"
The joy of understanding, caught at the exact moment ignorance ends. Nothing else on the site states the human realm's wisdom this purely.

★ The Darkling Thrush — Thomas Hardy
Bleak century's end, and a thrush singing of "some blessed Hope, whereof he knew / And I was unaware." Tentative because it's about the *limit* of understanding — hope arriving as evidence you don't yet have. Human realm at its most Melencolia-I. 		★★★ The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock — T. S. Eliot
"there will be time / To wonder, 'Do I dare?' and, 'Do I dare?'"
The specifically human pit: self-consciousness recursing until action is impossible. A hundred visions and revisions, the overwhelming question never asked. The realm's gift (reflection) become its trap. 		★★★ First Fig — Edna St. Vincent Millay
"My candle burns at both ends... it gives a lovely light!"
Four lines of why being human — mortal, doomed, intense — feels glamorous from inside. The seduction of the "right mix": enough suffering to make the pleasure mean something. 		★★★ This Is Just To Say — William Carlos Williams
Ordinary appetite, minor transgression, apology-that-isn't, and the plums were delicious. Daily human life at the scale it's actually lived — desire and conscience negotiating over the icebox.

★★ Birches — Robert Frost
"Earth's the right place for love: / I don't know where it's likely to go better."
Wanting to leave earth and be brought back — climbing *toward* heaven, not to it. The turtle-yoke teaching in a New England accent: this is the realm where the mix is right, so stay.
titans
(pride, envy, contest; wisdom of coordination, shared defense, calling the callous to task, competition calling you to your best self)		 ★★★ To be of use — Marge Piercy
People who "jump into work head first," who "harness themselves, an ox to a heavy cart" — coordination for things beyond you, submersion in the common task. The titan wisdom with the pride burned off.

★★★ Let America Be America Again — Langston Hughes
Calling the callous to task at national scale while refusing to abandon the shared value being betrayed — critique and allegiance in the same breath. Shared defense of shared values, exactly. 		★★★ Richard Cory — Edwin Arlington Robinson
Status envy's whole optics in sixteen lines: the glitter from below, the hollowness above, and no information passing between them until the last line. The comparison economy where everyone loses.

★★ My Last Duchess — Robert Browning
Pride at the level where another person's un-ranked warmth ("she liked whate'er she looked on") reads as insubordination and gets "stopped." The demon-realm endpoint: control replacing relationship entirely. 		★★★ Invictus — William Ernest Henley
"I am the master of my fate, / I am the captain of my soul"
Genuinely thrilling — that's the point. Unconquerable sovereign selfhood is the titan realm's best offer, and the poem shows why people take the deal. (Note the realm-tell: even the exit-shaped gods of this poem are "whatever gods may be," addressed as rivals.)

★★ The Charge of the Light Brigade — Alfred, Lord Tennyson
The glory of coordinated sacrifice, gorgeous and unquestioning — "theirs not to reason why" as a feature. Seduction by honor; the poem half-knows someone had blundered and salutes anyway. 		★★★ Mending Wall — Robert Frost
The mild daily titan level: boundary maintenance as an annual neighborly ritual, each keeping the wall between them as they go. Not war — just the low-grade territorial vigilance of "good fences," inherited and never re-examined.
deva
(bliss; appreciating experience; stabilizing; reveling in creation; flips into hell when it runs out)		 ★★★ Pied Beauty — Gerard Manley Hopkins
"Glory be to God for dappled things"
Appreciation as an act of praise for creation itself — dappled, fickle, freckled particulars, referred upward to what "fathers-forth." Appreciating experience *and* reveling in creation in eleven lines.

★★ From Blossoms — Li-Young Lee
Peaches taken fully in — "joy to joy... blossom to impossible blossom." ★★ instead of ★★★ only because its famous close (living "as if death were nowhere in the background") is the deva trap stated as the wish — the poem knows it, which is the wisdom. 		★★★ Sunday Morning — Wallace Stevens
"complacencies of the peignoir, and late / Coffee and oranges in a sunny chair"
Opens inside the deva realm — comfort holding off "the dark encroachment of that old catastrophe" — and then spends seven stanzas showing why a paradise without change can't satisfy: "Death is the mother of beauty." The diagnosis of the realm, written from its most comfortable chair. 		★★★ Kubla Khan — Samuel Taylor Coleridge
"For he on honey-dew hath fed, / And drunk the milk of Paradise"
The pleasure-dome decreed: creation as pure sovereign delight, and the visionary glamour of the one who's tasted it. That the vision famously broke off mid-composition is the deva realm's whole biography. 		★★★ I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud — William Wordsworth
"the bliss of solitude"
Mild resident-level deva: bliss encountered, then banked — the daffodils paying out later wealth whenever wanted. Appreciation with a stabilizing savings account.

★★ I dwell in Possibility (466) — Emily Dickinson
Living *in* the house of creation — more windows, superior doors, visitors the fairest — "spreading wide my narrow Hands / To gather Paradise." Reveling in creative capacity as a dwelling, at humane scale.

tag sweep — famous poems, quick realm tags

Since there's no site-native top-100, this is the standard most-anthologized set (nearly all on PF), tagged the way you suggested. Format: [tag] — reasoning in a phrase.

  • Song of Myself — Whitman — [all]: deliberately tours every realm and identifies with each ("I am the man, I suffer'd, I was there"); the bodhisattva move done in free verse.
  • The Waste Land — Eliot — [all]: hell (unreal city), preta (the typist scene's joyless appetite), animal (stupor), titans (falling towers), deva-in-ruins; ends reaching for the exit in Sanskrit.
  • Auguries of Innocence — Blake — [all]: "joy and woe are woven fine" is the realms' moral physics stated as couplets.
  • Harlem — Hughes — [preta/hell]: deferred wanting festering through every simile toward explosion.
  • Ozymandias — Shelley — [titans]: pride called to task by sand; the trap and its verdict in one sonnet.
  • The Second Coming — Yeats — [titans/hell]: coordination failing ("the centre cannot hold"), conviction maldistributed, something pitiless arriving.
  • The Tyger — Blake — [deva/hell]: reveling in creation, aimed at creation's terrifying half; the deva realm forced to look at hell and asked "did he who made the Lamb make thee?"
  • Do Not Go Gentle into That Good Night — Dylan Thomas — [hell/human]: righteous rage recommended as the correct response to dying — hell-wisdom deployed at the human realm's defining fact.
  • The Road Not Taken — Frost — [human]: choice, and the story-making about choice ("I shall be telling this with a sigh") — human-realm narrative machinery caught running.
  • Because I could not stop for Death (479) — Dickinson — [human]: mortality as a civil carriage ride through the whole arc of a life; the human realm's defining companion, met with courtesy.
  • "Hope" is the thing with feathers (314) — Dickinson — [human]: hope that never asks a crumb — pointedly not preta; the human realm's renewable resource.
  • Wild nights - Wild nights! (269) — Dickinson — [preta/deva]: luxury-craving, but "done with the compass" — desire imagining its own completion, which preta never actually reaches.
  • The Red Wheelbarrow — Williams — [none]: pure attention with no grasping, no aversion, no self in it; arguably what any realm looks like the moment you actually just look.
